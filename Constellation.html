What is a Constellation?

A constellation is a group of stars that form a recognizable pattern or shape in the night sky. These patterns often resemble animals, mythological characters, or objects, and they have been used for navigation and storytelling for thousands of years. Constellations are not physically connected â€” the stars in a constellation can be light years apart from each other.

Constellations are often formed by stars that are at different distances from Earth but appear close together when viewed from our perspective. Over time, astronomers have divided the sky into 88 modern constellations. Some of the most famous constellations include:

  • Orion: The hunter, home to the bright stars Betelgeuse and Rigel.
  • Ursa Major: Contains the Big Dipper, one of the most recognizable asterisms.
  • Lyra: A small constellation, home to the bright star Vega.
  • Canis Major: Contains Sirius, the brightest star in the night sky.

Sirius

Brightest star in the night sky, part of the Canis Major constellation.

Betelgeuse

A red supergiant in the Orion constellation, nearing the end of its life cycle.

Polaris

The North Star, part of the Ursa Minor constellation, used for navigation.

Alpha Centauri

The closest star system to Earth, located in the constellation Centaurus.

Vega

A bright star in the Lyra constellation, part of the Summer Triangle.

Spica

The brightest star in the Virgo constellation, a binary star system.
