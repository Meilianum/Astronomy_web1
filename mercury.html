MERCURY

Mercury, the smallest planet in the solar system, is the closest to the Sun, with a diameter of about 4,880 kilometers—slightly larger than Earth's Moon. Despite its small size, Mercury is crucial to understanding planetary formation and the dynamics of the inner solar system. The planet's surface is heavily cratered, resembling the Moon, indicating minimal geological activity over billions of years. Mercury has a thin atmosphere, composed mainly of oxygen, sodium, hydrogen, helium, and potassium, but it is too thin to support life or shield the surface from solar radiation or meteorite impacts. Mercury experiences extreme temperature variations due to its lack of atmosphere. Daytime temperatures can reach 430°C (800°F), while nighttime temperatures can plunge to -180°C (-290°F). This is because of its slow rotation; a single day on Mercury lasts 176 Earth days, but it takes only 88 Earth days to orbit the Sun. The planet has a weak magnetic field, about 1% the strength of Earth's, suggesting a partially molten core. Mercury has no moons, setting it apart from Earth and Venus. Despite its proximity to the Sun, Mercury is difficult to observe from Earth, best seen during twilight. NASA's MESSENGER mission has provided valuable insights into its surface, atmosphere, and magnetic field.
